Las Palmas Survival kit


Las Palmas de Gran Canaria, commonly known as Las Palmas, is one of the largest cities in Spain, and is the largest city in the Canary Islands. The population is 380 000 people, or roughly two Turkus.

Bus

directions_bus

Las Palmas has the best quality bus system in the archipelago and one of the best in Spain. The bright yellow buses are known simply as guaguas. The simple fare, paid directly to the driver, is 1.40e. A ticket good for 10 rides is available for 8,50e in any convenience store in the city. Official website for more info here. Change the language to English at the bottom of the page.

The most important bus lines have frequencies of between 3 and 15 minutes during the day and between 10 and 40 minutes at night. Most lines have service throughout the night. Among the most useful lines are:

  • Line 1 Teatro-Puerto
  • Line 12 Hoya de La Plata-Puerto
  • Line 17 Teatro-Auditorio

Taxi

local_taxi

You can hail any taxi you see in the street with the green light on, or you can call 928 460 000 and ask for one. Don't dare to First Card it, tho :)

Initial charge: 1,65e

Charge per kilometer: 0,60e

Safety

Las Palmas is a fairly safe city, and during the day you should be able walk around freely, but it is recommended to avoid the port's and Santa Catalina Park's area at night, specially during the weekend.

Working in Las Palmas

The goal is to work as normally as possible during the week. There are daily sessions, that will cut 30-60 minutes of your day. We acknowledge that some people might work a bit shorter days, and that's fine.

What's important is to avoid excess internal work, as that would rise the cost of this trip. For people mostly on the beach bench we hope to find meaningful work to advance common matters.

What is this?

It's a Avalon goes Las Palmas web app!

Well. At first I thought of creating a .pdf or similar to pass all the information along. But how would people carry those around then, anyway? Then I also realized how much fun it is sometimes to play around with text editors, and this was the result of an afternoon and a night. Of course I had help for the content from other organisers, whom I'm much grateful of.

If it doesn't work perfectly on your Android or whatever device, I'd first suggest to get a proper phone for yourself. If that's not doable, please contribute to the code. And trust me, this code you wouldn't want to show to clients.
